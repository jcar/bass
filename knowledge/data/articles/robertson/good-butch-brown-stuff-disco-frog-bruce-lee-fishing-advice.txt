Loooong article, cool to see that about one of our drywallin' bassin' brothers. Some random stuff that stood out to me:
> He calls up the lunar chart to make sure the moon isn't in the daytime sky. "If the moon is out, I have better things to do."
> "What I've done is gone to imitation bluegill baits. That's my own creation. Not on the market. You gotta match what they're eating. That's the best way to trick a trophy fish." [He pours his own to get the color that is working on any given day.]
> "I'd take the time to tweak my bait and make it look anatomically correct. I'd paint my hooks to match the baits. A stealth presentation is key."
> Brown has several boats, but his favorite...is a 14' Alumacraft he saw for sale...when he was 16. Cost: $400. Brown's 1966 model is leaky, scratched up, patched up and painted black numerous times over....
> "It doesn't spook the fish as it sits low in the water. That keeps me out of their sightline as they follow the bait back to the boat. They're not afraid of my boat. It looks like a beat-up log." [Believe the lakes he fishes are 'lectric-only? Or 9.9 hp or less?]
> "Butch has a complete understanding of the forage in his water and works with our production team to get the color perfect," [Deps CEO Kazumasa] Okumura says. "He will not substitute for perfection.
> "I truly respect his style of fishing for only trophy fish. He truly is the honmono [real deal] big fish hunter."
> "I go fishing a lot and I catch nothing. I go weeks...."
Did he ever have a world record on? Possibly:
> Over the years Brown has hooked and lost 3 fish he's confident were over 20 lbs. In 2005 Brown had an unprecedented 128 double-digit largemouth in 9 months' time, but it also included a gut punch....
> "The one I shouldn't have lost was on a Huddleston swimbait. I've seen 20-lb fish so I knew this was a big one. I could tell by the features, the big head, the size of the eyeballs, the big body. I had the heavy rod and I kept her coming." He had the fish ready to net "but giants like to dig down and – bam! – she snapped me right off. I was sick to my stomach."

Cool little spring Carolina rig tip from Alton Jones Sr.
From an older Western Bass post (sorry can't link it, it's at westernbass com):
> "Most fishermen think of using a Carolina rig in deeper water. It works there for sure, but I like to use it in the shallows in the springtime. I will catch a lot of fish up shallow on this rig.
> "It's ideal when your fish a row of docks. Put your flipping gear down and pick up the Carolina rig to fish in the stretch where there are no docks. Look for points or other hard bottom areas like seawalls.
> "Use a heavy weight like a 3/4 or a 1-oz. Since I'm fishing a good distance from the fish, I like the weight so it makes a splash in the shallows. This isn't pitching where the fish see you, so the natural instinct is to investigate the noise, the splash and then the heavy weight stirring up the bottom. It is a great big-fish option. So simple, but so effective."
Sold! Imma try it!
